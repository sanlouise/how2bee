Beekeeping has been around for over 5,000 years, becoming essential to ancient Egypt, ancient China, ancient Greece, and many other civilizations along the way. Honey has long been used as a sugar substitute, especially since many civilizations did not have access to sugar. On top of that, the golden substance we've all come to know and love has been used in cosmetics, medicines, and even to make alcoholic beverages (particularly in Greece around 2000 BC).[1] In light of the influence honey has had on civilization, it makes sense that some of history's more influential figures would have also had a fascination with bees.

Although this list includes only a few of the famous individuals throughout history who kept bees, some of the names may surprise you! Here's the list of famous beekeepers (or bee enthusiasts!) in no particular order…

10. Aristotle

Aristotle was very passionate about apiculture, honey bees, and he worked closely together with countless beekeepers. In fact, he made several theories about the behavior and even the origin of bees. For example, he thought bees came from the flowers of brooms, reeds, and olives! While this may sound ridiculous to us (and it is a bit ridiculous), the reason why he believed this is quite interesting.

First, he noticed that every time there was a large harvest of olives there were also significantly more bees swarming. This led him to believe that bees spontaneously generate from flowers. But many of his other observations were more accurate.

He also noticed that the more the flower Thyme bloomed, the better the honey yield beekeepers could expect, which allowed Aristotle to forecast honey yields. But his observations did not stop there, as he then developed an entire list of flowers that bees thrive on (and that make delicious honey) when placed around their hives.

[IMG]

In addition, Aristotle observed that bees flew only to a single species of plant for each individual flight and even described bees obtaining pollen (which he mistakenly called wax) from flowers with their front feet and pushing it to their hind legs. He also believed that honey was gathered from bees' mouths and vomited into the cells of the honey comb.

Unfortunately, his last conclusion was that honey combs came from tree gum and that honey is created by something that falls from the air. Sadly, Aristotle never understood the correlation between bees, pollination, and flowering, but he was still deeply intrigued by them![2]

One thing is for certain - Aristotle loved bees much more than hornets and wasps.

"Concerning the generation of animals akin to them, as hornets and wasps, the facts in all cases are similar to a certain extent, but are devoid of the extraordinary features which characterize bees; this we should expect, for they have nothing divine about them as the bees have." ~ Aristotle 384 BC – 322 BC

9. Thomas Jefferson

Apis mellifera, or what we call the 'honey bee,' first arrived in North America around the 1600s together with European colonists. The New World was known as the land of milk and honey but, as the New World did not naturally have bees, European settlers brought honey bees to ensure the ideal remained (honey is delectable after all). The honey bee began to spread throughout the New World as quickly as European settlements expanded into the new territory.[3]

[IMG]

Edmund Bacon (1785-1866), Thomas Jefferson's overseer, is known to have been a beekeeper. While Thomas Jefferson himself was in all likelihood not a beekeeper, he did comment a few times about bees, beekeeping, and honey. 'Honey was a better substitute for sugar than any other preparation.'

Another famous comment made by Thomas Jefferson is quite interesting. Native Americans called honey bees the white man's fly and 'considered their approach as indicating the approach of the settlements of the whites.'[4] Thus, it could even be argued that apiculture was widespread among European settlers when expanding into the New World as honey was the perfect substitute for sugar at the time.

8. George Washington

As the first President of the United States, one would think George Washington was entirely too busy to concern himself with bees. But he wasn't. In 1787, President Washington even had an apiary constructed on the Mount Vernon property that he had inherited. Today, a replica sits in the place of the beehives on the grounds of Mount Vernon.

Here's a fun fact. President Washington's hives, along with many of the beehives in the southern states in the late 1700s, were made out of black gum from trees. The reason for this was because the heartwood decays quickly, which leaves a hollow trunk that is perfect as a beehive. These black gum beehives became known as 'gums,' which is why the word is now used generally for any hive.[5]

7. Napoleon Bonaparte

Napoleon Bonaparte is one of the most famous French military and political leaders in history. Apart from expanding France's political boundaries with multiple wars, Napoleon also deeply desired for France's culture to grow and flourish. And he did this with the symbol of an insect — the honey bee.

The 'Golden Bee' was Napoleon's and France's emblem during the Napoleonic era. In 1804, when Napoleon was crowned Emperor of the French, he wore purple robes that were embroidered everywhere with the 'Golden Bee' symbol. Napoleon respected bees as they were orderly, efficient, and worked without question for the good of the colony. Many believe he desired the French to be the same.[6]

6. Ben Franklin

Legend has it that Benjamin Franklin briefly became passionate about beekeeping. As for many beginner beekeepers, Ben was not fond of the idea of getting stung by a bee and, back in the 18th Century, getting stung was almost certain for beekeepers as bee suits were not widely used.

So Ben went to the drawing board and began to develop a piece of equipment that would forever change beekeeping – the crotchless bee suit.

Crotchless? Apparently, Ben suffered from massive bladder stones in the years leading up to his death in 1790. Perhaps because of this (and perhaps not), Ben designed his bee suit to allow for quick urination whenever the need arose.

What Ben may not have expected, however, is that after the first time he used it a bee landed on his… well, you get the point. After this rather uncomfortable experience, Ben forgot about the bee suit and instead invented the first medical catheter. While bees probably were not Ben's forte, beekeeping may have influenced his other more notable inventions that changed the world as we know it.[7]

'Tart words make no friends; a spoonful of honey will catch more flies than a gallon of vinegar.' ~ Benjamin Franklin

5. Thomas Edison

Thomas Edison is famous for his thousands of inventions, including the light bulb – but he's not famous for beekeeping. Nonetheless, his estate in Fort Myers, Florida, has kept and continues to keep bees in order to pollinate the numerous flowers on the property. Although this does not necessarily confirm that Thomas Edison was a beekeeper, there's a good chance he studied them due to his curious nature.[8]

4. Sherlock Holmes

Sir Conan Doyle, the author of the Sherlock Holmes series, is an amateur detective and an excellent novelist to say the least. However, he is also an avid bee enthusiast.

Many fans of Sherlock Holmes know that a few years after Doyle attempted to kill Holmes at the Reichenback Falls, Doyle instead decided to send Holmes into retirement in Sussex where he would spend the rest of his days as a beekeeper. It's rather easy to envision Holmes conducting more than a few experiments on his beloved bees, but we at How 2 Bee are certain that they are experiments to help the bees.[9]

3. Jon Bon Jovi

It's fairly well-known that Jon Bon Jovi is also a famous beekeeper. But there may be more than meets the eye as to why he does.

New Jersey. 1964. As the state attempted to encourage more farming, New Jersey granted tax exemptions (up to 98% in some cases) to citizens that produced more than $500 in agricultural revenue from more than five acres of land. But as of August, 2015, that subsidy changed.

Now, New Jersey grants tax exemptions only to citizens that make more than $1,000 in agricultural revenue. Not only that, but now citizens that want to qualify must also submit proof of farming revenue and agree to being inspected regularly. Although the increase of this agricultural revenue needed for the tax exemptions is not much, the change seeks to separate the fake farmers (and rockstars!) from the real ones.

Jon Bon Jovi is probably living on much more than just a prayer, as he paid only $104 on 7.1 acres of land in 2010, part of which was used to keep bees.[10]
