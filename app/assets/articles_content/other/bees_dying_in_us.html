So let's give a bit of background to the problem, the impact of bees dying, and the top 6 causes for bees dying. We'll even offer a potential solution at the end, which could seriously save the bees with your help.

The Rise of a Serious Problem

Bee populations have been declining steadily in the U.S. for more than a decade. The current crisis dates back to the Autumn season in 2006 when beekeepers around the United States reported massive losses in bee populations. During this period, the U.S. lost an average of more than 30% of its hives and up to 90% in some cases. Although it varied case-by-case, bees would typically leave their hives to forage but they would never come back.

Beekeepers in many regions were finding empty hives except for a few adult bees and a live queen. In most of these cases, there were no bee corpses remaining to tell the tale of what happened. The losses were fast and unprecedented. Scientists rushed to research and even recommend solutions to solve the issue of why bees were dying. But, although there were numerous recommendations, very little was done in the U.S. to solve the growing problem.

European beekeepers experienced the same problem with bees dying around the same time. Unlike the U.S., the European Union (EU) was quick to take serious steps. For example, the EU has already announced a ban on a new class of pesticides known as neonicotinoids. According to the EU, neonicotinoids are responsible for most cases on declining bee populations in Europe.[1]

[IMG]

The U.S. hasn't taken similar measures despite many environmental and consumer groups as well as U.S. beekeeping associations who have already sued the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for failing to do enough to safeguard bees from harmful pesticides.

It wasn't until May, 2015 that the U.S. government finally gave the subject of declining bee populations the seriousness it deserves. The Obama Administration recently announced plans to make millions of acres of federal land more friendly to bees. The administration has also promised to increase research funding and put in stringent measures to discourage use of pesticides.[2] Although most people welcome this move, some argue that it's too late considering the damage is already done and bees face other enemies today.

The most recent studies on the causes of bees dying in the U.S. today reveal that a disease known as colony collapse disorder (CCD) is in fact more deadly than pesticides when it comes to the major causes of Wintering Bees, according to the latest honey bee population survey conducted by the AIA (Apiary Inspectors of America) and the USDA (U.S. Department of Agriculture).[3] Making more land available for beekeeping may not, therefore, be the solution to the increase of bees dying in the U.S.

Most beekeepers consider a 15% loss in their hive population to be acceptable every year, especially when wintering their bees (linked to above). In the U.S., honey bees are still fighting for their lives as honey bee colonies are weaker now more than ever before in the U.S. Only time will tell whether the $3 million USDA subsidy offered in early 2014 will help to increase the honey bee populations in America.

The program is meant to attract cattle ranchers and Midwest dairy farmers to reseed their farms with bee-friendly crops like clover and alfalfa which offer perfect habitats for honey bees to thrive.

By the way, this isn't the first time the U.S. faces a problem involving bees dying. The problem can be traced back to the 1880's as well as the 1920's and the 1960's. The U.S. also experienced declining bee population problems in the mid 1990's where Pennsylvania beekeepers lost more than 50% of their bee colonies without one clear cause. During this period, a Top Bee Diseases and Predators (in particular, the tracheal and Varroa mites) hit honey bees hard.

These parasites continue to affect honey bees today, which have certainly given many beekeepers a few sleepless nights. The sudden amount of bees dying in the U.S. can't be attributed to solely one cause. Many factors have contributed to this problem, including habitat loss and global warming, although pesticides and CCD are among the main causes of declining honey bee populations in the U.S.

Bees Dying and What it Means for You

You may be asking yourself, "why is the U.S. and the world at large even worried about declining bee populations?" Well, the answer is that bees play crucial roles in our daily life, particularly because they are pollinators. The large quantity of bees dying are causing an unprecedented pollination crisis. Below is a brief discussion about this impact and what this means for you.

An Unprecedented Pollination Crisis

Western nations like the U.S. rely heavily on bees to drive the commercial agriculture industry. According to the most recent USDA studies, honey bees contribute more than $15 billion of America's commercial food production.[4]

This represents approximately one third of all food produced in the U.S. These foods include some of our most favorite summer fruits like strawberries and blueberries as well as guar beans (which are used in countless products) and many other types of food - and all of them depend on pollination. On average, bees are responsible for pollinating approximately 100 key crops, and these crops total approximately 90% of the U.S. nutrition.[5]

Let's give a quick scenario. If there were no bees in the U.S., there would be a pollination crisis that could easily lead to a shortage in fresh fruits, vegetables, and nuts, which are heavily dependent on insect pollination. Although some people may argue that there are other ways of pollinating crops that don't rely on bees, the U.S. stands to lose out on the production of many healthy foods like cherries, broccoli, onions, among many other types of produce. This will happen as the increase of bees dying forces the rise of other pollination methods.

And You Thought 2008 Was Bad?

Another significant impact of bees dying in the U.S. is increased food prices. Bees pollinate crops for free. Artificial pollination methods result in increased food production costs, which eventually results in increased food prices.

The U.S. might even be forced to increase imports of some foods to sustain demand if honey bee populations keep declining. This would also result in increased food prices.

The cost of beekeeping has also increased drastically as a result of declining bee populations. Even though the USDA is giving incentives to promote beekeeping, honey bees in the U.S. are having trouble finding enough to forage. As a result, beekeepers must purchase more bee food to sustain their bees when their colony can't produce enough honey to survive throughout the seasons.[6]

This might come as a surprise, but bees dying also has a negative impact on employment. Bees employ many people - beekeepers, sellers of beekeeping supplies and equipment, farmers who rely on bee pollination, people who sell honey and other bee products, among other professions. Therefore, an increase in bees dying in the U.S. can result in significant job losses in the industry and other related industries since bees create thousands of jobs. The economic effect of there being no honey bees in the U.S. is catastrophic from an employment standpoint.

It's also important to remember that honey bees contribute more than $15 billion to the U.S. economy through their vital role of keeping fresh fruits, vegetables, and nuts in our diets.

Do You Feel... Unhealthy?

Considering honey bees are responsible for producing approximately 90% of the U.S. nutrition, a steady increase in bees dying would obviously result in nutrition problems for millions of Americans. Fresh fruits, vegetables, and nuts are some of the most nutritious foods and bees are responsible for pollinating most (if not all) of these foods.

If there were no bees in the U.S., most fresh fruit, vegetables, and nuts produced wouldn't meet the nutritional threshold for healthy foods even if other pollination methods were used. This could then lead to an increase in medical problems.

National Food Security Problem

Insect pollination of plants, flowers, trees, (which is done mostly by bees) is crucial to U.S. food security. Honey bees enable the production of approximately 100 crops in the U.S. Globally, honey bees are responsible for pollinating around 35% of all crops. If there continues to be an increase in bees dying, this would lead to food security problems in the U.S. and potentially the world at large.

Top 6 Reasons for Bees Dying

There are many reasons behind decreasing bee populations in the U.S. and worldwide. These reasons range from pesticides to common honey bee diseases to problems like Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD). Below is a list of the top 6 reasons, in no particular order, for a sharp increase of bees dying in the U.S.

Pesticides

Many studies indicate that pesticides are a major cause of bees dying in the U.S. However, most pesticides aren't a direct cause of bees dying. Many studies indicate that pesticides kill bees indirectly - prolonged exposure to pesticides cause bees to become more susceptible to other bee diseases. One such study indicated that bees exposed to lethal doses of pesticides, such as neonicotinoids, for a prolonged period of time become easily infected by a common bee parasite: Nosema.

Neonicotinoids have also been found to fog the brains of honey bees altering their behavior in the process. For instance, neonicotinoids have also been found to keep honey bees from supplying their beehives with enough food to sustain the hives, often times causing the collapse of the colony.[7]

Harsh Chemicals

Pesticides aren't the only chemicals responsible for bees dying in the U.S. Bees come into contact with many chemicals today. For instance, commercial beehives are fumigated regularly with chemicals to keep off mites, among other destructive hive insects and pests. Bees also come into direct contact with many genetically modified crops today, which are packed with chemicals resulting in the spread of pollen that has significantly less nutritional value.

Many studies suggest that the build-up of genetically modified pollen as well as synthetic chemicals is stressing bee populations across the U.S., which in the end cause an increase of bees dying. This theory has been universally accepted since organic bee colonies that aren't exposed to chemicals and genetically modified crops experience a survival rate much better than that of regular honey bee colonies.[8]

Furthermore, typical bee pollen today contains over 150 different chemical residues from pesticides and other chemicals which are inevitable for profitable agribusiness. In a nutshell, the current agricultural system's use of chemicals must change to increase bee populations.

[IMG]

Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD)

CCD is also responsible for bees dying in the U.S to a great extent. CCD is characterised by bee colonies disappearing abruptly. There are many causes of CCD with the most notable being pesticides (neonicotinoids), Varroa mite infections, malnutrition, pathogens, and genetic factors (such as immuno-deficiencies).

Some studies also indicate that CCD is caused by changing beekeeping practices or a combination of two or more of the above factors. However, the search for a specific cause of CCD is still ongoing. Scientists are looking for a commonality that will lead to a specific cause.[9]

Radiation

Radiation has also been linked to the increase of bees dying in the U.S. Bees are known to be vulnerable to atmospheric electromagnetic radiation, which is increasing as the number of mobile phones and wireless communication towers increases. The radiation theory suggests that radiation interferes with the navigation systems of bees preventing them from being able to find their way back to their hives. As improbable as this theory seems, there is evidence backing this theory which has also been linked to CCD.[10]

Climate Change

Studies also suggest that global warming is partly to blame for a significant number of bees dying in the U.S. and the world at large. This may be attributed to the fact that global warming has increased the growth rate of pathogens like viruses, mites, and fungi known to attack bee colonies. The unusual hot and cold winter weather fluctuations experienced in the recent years may also be affecting bee populations negatively given the fact that bees thrive in consistent weather patterns.[11]

Habitat Loss

Most of the suitable natural habitats for bees in North America has been transformed into industrial parks, office complexes, houses, highways, and malls. Urbanization causes bee deaths by destroying bee habitats directly. Urbanization also isolates and fragments the land where bees used to forage. In turn, this reduces the genetic diversity of certain bee species, which eventually results in bees dying since bees have a harder time getting mates. Also, the resulting bees are more susceptible to diseases among other problems since they aren't genetically diverse.[12]

A Potential Solution: Make Your Own Personal Apiary!

How Apiaries Help in Increasing Honey Bee Populations

An apiary is another name for a bee yard where honey bees are Your Beekeeping Supplies & Equipment. Beekeepers can set up apiaries on their own farms, rent land to set up apiaries, and many do it in the middle of the city. Some farmers provide land for setting up apiaries for free because they stand to enjoy pollination benefits. Others ask for honey or money as rent for using the land.

Apiaries are helping to increase the population of honey bees in the U.S. simply because they provide perfect environments for honey bees to thrive. The $3 million USDA subsidy offered in early 2014 is encouraging cattle ranchers and Midwest dairy farmers to set up apiaries while also encouraging farmers to reseed with bee-friendly crops like clover and alfalfa, among other bee-friendly crops to create perfect habitats for honey bees to thrive.

The more people that become beekeepers, the more people will better understand what is causing the increase of bees dying, which means the better we will be able to solve the problem. Honey bees need your help. Even one person can make a difference, and it could even be a fun problem to solve when you start your own apiary.

Setting up an Apiary: What you Need to Know

Setting up an apiary is highly recommendable, especially if you are in some of the best honey production areas in the U.S., such as Texas, Florida, California, and the Upper-Midwest areas. Nevertheless, you can set up an apiary anywhere in the U.S. - even in the city if you have enough space.

Setting up an apiary is highly recommended if you want to make a positive contribution to pollination. You can also set up an apiary to benefit financially from paid pollination in areas like California, the Great Lakes, the Pacific Northwest, and the Northeast regions. You may consider setting up an apiary for other reasons, such as queen bee mating and rearing.

The factors to consider when setting up an apiary aren't very different from the factors to consider when setting up typical beehives. For instance, you need to choose a site with plenty of pollen and nectar, particularly if you want to set up your apiary in the city or a place with scarce pollen and nectar. Apiaries also need an adequate supply of clean water. Refer to our articles on How to Start a Beehive and how to start beekeeping for more information.

Nevertheless, there are some import factors you must consider when setting up an apiary. For instance, the number of hives that should be placed in an apiary varies depending on factors like the size of the area, the nectar and pollen sources, the bee species, etc. If an apiary has too many hives, there will be stiff competition for resources, which will lead to low honey yields as well as a higher disease transmission rate.

Apiaries should have 25 to 40 hives, although migrating beekeepers can have more hives in one apiary located in an area with favorable nectar flow (places where a large amount of nectar is produced by plants, flowers, trees, among others). However, the general rule of thumb is one hive per acre.

With the above guidelines and information from our other beekeeping articles and beekeeping books, you shouldn't have a problem starting an apiary. Together we can help out the bees, help solve the problem of bees dying in the U.S., and even enjoy some sweet delicious honey in the meantime.
